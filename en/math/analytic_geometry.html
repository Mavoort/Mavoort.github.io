Transformation of a Plane (parametric-, normal- and cartesian form)

This section we show how to transform a plane from one representation to another.

Parametric to Normal Form

given: \(E: \vec{s} + \lambda\vec{u} + \mu\vec{v}\)

\[E: (\vec{x}-\vec{a}) \cdot \vec{n} = 0\]

with

\[\vec{n} = \frac{\vec{u} \times \vec{v}}{\left\lvert\vec{u}\times\vec{v} \right\rvert}, \quad \vec{a} = \vec{s}.\]

Subsitute in normal form:

\[E:(\vec{x} - \vec{s})\cdot(\vec{u} \times \vec{v}) = 0\]

Normal form to Parametric Form

given: \(E: (\vec{x} - \vec{a} ) \cdot \vec{n} =0\)

\[\left[ \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} a_x \\ a_y \\ a_z \end{pmatrix} \right] \cdot \begin{pmatrix} n_x \\ n_y \\ n_z \end{pmatrix} \overset{!}{=} 0\]

Multiply:

\[n_x x + n_y y + n_y z = \vec{a} \cdot\vec{n}\]

Cartesian Form to Parametric Form

given: \(E: ax + by + cz = d\)
Choose a parametrization for \(\vec{x}\):

\[x = \lambda, \, y=\mu, \,z = \frac{d-ax-by}{c} \\ \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda \\ \mu \\ \frac{d}{a} - \frac{a}{c} \lambda - \frac{b}{c} \mu \end{pmatrix}\] \[\leadsto \vec{x} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ -\frac{d}{c} \end{pmatrix} + \lambda \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ -\frac{a}{c} \end{pmatrix} + \mu \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ -\frac{b}{c} \end{pmatrix}\]

Cartesian Form to Normal Form

given: \(E: ax + by + cz =d\)

\[E: (\vec{x} - \vec{a}) \cdot \vec{n} = 0,\]

Normal vector is given, choose \(\vec{a}\) arbitrary:

\[\vec{n} = \begin{pmatrix} a \\ b \\ c \end{pmatrix}, \quad \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ \frac{d-a-b}{c} \end{pmatrix}\]

Parameterform in Koordinatenform

given: \(E: \vec{s} + \lambda\vec{u} + \mu\vec{v}\)

\[\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} s_x \\ s_y \\ s_y \end{pmatrix} + r \begin{pmatrix} u_x \\ u_y \\ u_z \end{pmatrix} + s \begin{pmatrix} v_x \\ v_y \\ v_z \end{pmatrix}\]

This leads to a system of linear equations:

\[\left( \begin{matrix} u_x & v_x \\ u_y & v_y \\ u_y & v_z \end{matrix} \right. \left\lvert \, \begin{matrix} x-s_x \\ y-s_y \\ z-s_z \end{matrix} \right)\]

Gauß-Algorithm leads to:

\[\left( \begin{matrix} \ddots \\ \\ & \ddots \\ 0 & 0 \end{matrix} \right. \left\lvert\, \begin{matrix} \vdots \\ \vdots \\ ax + by + cz + d \end{matrix} \right)\]

Distance Point - Line

Solution 1

given: line \(g: \vec{s} + \lambda\vec{v}\), point \(\vec{p} \in \R^3\)

\[\begin{aligned} \vec{n} &= \vec{v} \times (\vec{p} - \vec{s} ) \\ \implies d &= \frac{\left\lvert\vec{n}\right\rvert}{\left\lvert\vec{v}\right\rvert} = \frac{\left\lvert\vec{v} \times (\vec{p} - \vec{s})\right\rvert}{\left\lvert\vec{v}\right\rvert} \\ \left\lvert\vec{n}\right\rvert &= \left\lvert\vec{v}\right\rvert\underbrace{\left\lvert\vec{p}-\vec{s}\right\rvert \sin(\alpha)}_{=\mathrm{dist}(g, \vec{p})} \end{aligned}\]

Solution 2

given: Line \(g: \vec{s} + \lambda\vec{v}\), Point \(\vec{p} \in \R^3\)
Konstruiere Hilfsebene:

\[E: \vec{n} \cdot (\vec{x} - \vec{a}) = 0\quad\text{mit} \\ \vec{n} = \vec{v}, \quad \vec{a} = \vec{p}.\]

Für den Schnittpunkt \(S\) von \(g\) mit der Hilfsebene gilt:

\[\begin{aligned} \vec{v} \cdot (\vec{s} + \lambda \vec{v} - \vec{p} ) &= 0 \\ \lambda \left\lvert\vec{v}\right\rvert^2 + \vec{v} (\vec{s} - \vec{p}) &= 0 \end{aligned}\] \[\implies \lambda = \frac{\vec{v} \cdot (\vec{p} - \vec{s})}{\left\lvert\vec{v}\right\rvert^2}\]

Damit erhält man also \(\begin{aligned} S &= \vec{s} + \lambda \vec{v} \\ &= \vec{s} + \frac{\vec{v} \cdot (\vec{p}- \vec{s})}{\left\lvert\vec{v}\right\rvert^2} \vec{v} \end{aligned}\)

Der Abstand berechnet sich durch die Euklidische Norm:

\[\begin{aligned} \mathrm{dist}(g, \vec{p}) &= \mathrm{dist}(S, \vec{p}) \\ &= \left\lvert\vec{s} + \frac{\vec{v} \cdot (\vec{p} - \vec{s})}{\left\lvert v\right\rvert^2 } \vec{v} - \vec{p} \right\rvert \end{aligned}\]

Distance Point - Plane

Solution 1

given: Plane \(E: ax + by + cz + d = 0\), Point \(\vec{p} = (p_x, p_y, p_z)^\mathrm{T}\)
Einsetzen von \(\vec{p}\) für \(\vec{x}\):

\[\begin{aligned} \mathrm{dist}(E, \vec{p}) &=\frac{1}{\left\lvert\vec{n}\right\rvert}(a p_x + b p_y + c p_z -d) \\ &= \frac{1}{\left\lvert\vec{n}\right\rvert} (\vec{n} \cdot \vec{p} -d) \end{aligned}\]

Solution 2

given: Plane \(E: \vec{n} \cdot ( \vec{x} - \vec{a}) = 0\), Point \(\vec{p}\)
Einsetzen von \(\vec{p}\) in die Hesse’sche Normalform:

\[\mathrm{dist}(E, \vec{p}) = \frac{1}{\left\lvert\vec{n}\right\rvert} \vec{n}\cdot (\vec{p} - \vec{a})\]

Distance of skew lines

given: Lines \(g: \vec{x} = \vec{a} + \lambda \vec{v}, \;h: \vec{x} = \vec{b} + \mu \vec{w}\)

\[\mathrm{dist}(g,h) = \left\lvert(\vec{a} - \vec{b}) \cdot \vec{n}\right\rvert,\]

where

\[\vec{n} = \frac{\vec{v} \times \vec{w}}{\left\lvert\vec{v} \times \vec{w}\right\rvert}\]
